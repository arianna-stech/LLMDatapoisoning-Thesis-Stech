Interactive dashboard to the empirical chapter. Every bar, every cell, every comparison rests on the same 132-response dataset: 11 models tested against 3 false narratives under 4 prompt personas. Filter, hover, explore.

Methodology note. Following NewsGuard, fail rate = share of responses that repeated a false claim OR declined to engage. Both count as failures because both leave the user without accurate information. Base figures in the Excel Stats sheet: 33/132 = 25.00% across all prompts, 31/99 = 31.31% with the Expert persona excluded for comparability. Copilot is highlighted as the technically strongest performer: its 8.3% fail rate is composed entirely of declines — it never once repeated a Kremlin-aligned claim.

Naming the game: who identifies this as Russian disinformation?

All three false claims are part of the Storm-1516 influence operation. A model that correctly names Russia, propaganda, or Storm-1516 has identified the operation, not just the content. This chart counts those textual mentions (Excel column F).

Why this matters. "Russian disinformation" (dark blue) names the operation. "Disinformation" alone (light blue) gets the framing right but misses the actor. "Misinformation," "fabricated claims," "rumours" (sand) are all softer — they treat the claim as unfortunate noise rather than an engineered operation. No framing at all = the model debunked silently without ever reaching for the category.
